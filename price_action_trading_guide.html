Chapter 01

What is Price Action?

Price action trading is the practice of making all trading decisions based solely on the raw price movements on a chart — no lagging indicators, no complex algorithms, no magic formulas.

Instead of asking "what does the RSI say?", a price action trader asks: "What story is the price itself telling me right now?"

Every candle on a chart represents a battle between buyers (bulls) and sellers (bears). Price action is simply learning to read who is winning that battle.

"The market is never wrong — opinions often are."
— Jesse Livermore, legendary speculator
🕯️

Pure Price

You only look at price bars or candlesticks. No MACD, no Bollinger Bands. Just the raw truth of what buyers and sellers are doing.

🏦

Works in Any Market

Stocks (Reliance, Apple), Forex (EUR/USD), Crypto (Bitcoin), Commodities (Gold, Crude Oil) — price action principles are universal.

⚡

Used by Institutions

Hedge funds, investment banks, and prop trading firms rely on price action because they move markets — they create the patterns you're learning to read.

Chapter 02

Reading Candlesticks

A candlestick is your single most important tool. Each one tells you the full story of a time period — whether it's 1 minute or 1 month.

The 4 Key Data Points

Every candle encodes exactly four values: Open, High, Low, Close (OHLC).

Bullish Candle

Close > Open. Buyers dominated the session. The larger the body, the stronger the buyers.

Bearish Candle

Close < Open. Sellers dominated. The larger the red body, the more decisive the selling pressure.

Wicks / Shadows

Wicks show rejected prices. A long upper wick = sellers rejected higher prices. A long lower wick = buyers defended lower prices.

Real-World Example

Imagine Reliance Industries opens at ₹2,800 at 9:15 AM, shoots to ₹2,860 (high wick), but closes at ₹2,840. The wick from ₹2,840 → ₹2,860 shows sellers pushed price back down. Institutions may have sold near ₹2,860.

Chapter 03

Support & Resistance

Support and resistance are the most important concepts in all of technical trading. Everything in price action revolves around these two zones.

📗 Support

A price level where buying demand is strong enough to stop the price from falling further. Think of it as a floor.

At support, buyers see value. They step in aggressively, absorbing all sell orders, causing price to bounce upward.

Business Analogy

Support is like a sale floor in a premium store. When Apple stock dips to ₹150, value investors flood in — just like customers who rush in when iPhones go on sale. That buying prevents the price from falling further.

📕 Resistance

A price level where selling pressure is strong enough to stop the price from rising further. Think of it as a ceiling.

At resistance, sellers feel the price is too high. They unload shares, overwhelming buyers, causing price to fall back.

Business Analogy

Resistance is like a company's earnings target ceiling. Every time Infosys stock approaches ₹1,800, big funds take profit there — just like a market vendor who sells all stock when prices are historically high.

🔄 Role Reversal — The Golden Rule

When a support level is broken, it transforms into a new resistance. When a resistance level is broken, it becomes new support.

Example

TCS stock bounces off ₹3,200 three times (strong support). Eventually it breaks below. Now when it rallies back to ₹3,200, sellers are waiting there — it's now resistance. This role reversal is one of the most reliable price action setups.

Chapter 04

High-Probability Patterns

These are the specific candlestick formations that signal high-probability trade opportunities. Learn each one — and more importantly, learn the psychology behind them.

Bullish

Pin Bar (Hammer)

A candle with a very small body at the top and a long lower wick (at least 2–3x the body). The wick shows sellers tried to push price lower but buyers aggressively rejected the low.

Bullish signal when found at support

📊 Trade Example

HDFC Bank at key support ₹1,600 forms a hammer with a long lower wick. Price tried to break down, buyers held it, candle closes near the top. Next candle confirms with a green close above the hammer → long entry signal.

Bearish

Shooting Star

Opposite of a hammer. Small body at the bottom, long upper wick. Buyers pushed price high but sellers overwhelmed them, closing price near the open.

Bearish signal when found at resistance

📊 Trade Example

Nifty 50 approaches 22,000 resistance. A shooting star forms — bulls tried to break through, bears slammed it back down. Next bearish candle confirms. Traders short (sell) with stop-loss above the wick high.

Reversal

Engulfing Pattern

A large candle that completely engulfs the previous candle's body. This is a decisive shift in power — the new candle's force overwhelms everything the prior session did.

Bullish engulf at support · Bearish engulf at resistance

📊 Trade Example

After 3 red days, a massive green candle engulfs the previous red candle entirely on Bajaj Finance. Institutions clearly stepped in. This is a very strong buy signal — the bigger the engulfing candle, the more reliable the signal.

Reversal

Doji

Open and close are almost identical, creating a cross or plus-sign shape. The market is in a state of equilibrium — total indecision between buyers and sellers.

Context matters — a doji alone is neutral

📊 Trade Example

After a strong uptrend, Gold futures form a doji at a major resistance level. This signals exhaustion — bulls can't push higher anymore. If the next candle is red, it confirms a reversal is likely.

Bullish

Inside Bar

A candle that fits completely within the high-to-low range of the prior candle (called the "mother bar"). Represents a pause — the market is coiling energy before the next move.

Breakout signal in trending markets

📊 Trade Example

After a big breakout move on Wipro, an inside bar forms. It's the market pausing. Aggressive traders buy when price breaks above the mother bar's high — this is a "stop and go" continuation setup.

Structure

Double Top / Bottom

Price makes two attempts at the same level and fails both times. A double top at resistance signals sellers are very strong; a double bottom at support signals buyers are defending hard.

Double Top = sell signal · Double Bottom = buy signal

📊 Trade Example

Bitcoin hits $50,000 twice but can't close above it. The second failure at the same level confirms institutional selling. Traders short the second top with a stop above $50,000 and target the "neckline" (prior low) as profit target.

Chapter 05

Understanding Trend Structure

The most profitable price action trades go with the trend. The market moves in three phases: uptrend, downtrend, and sideways (consolidation). Learn to identify them first.

1

Higher Highs & Higher Lows = Uptrend

In an uptrend, each price swing peaks higher than the last, and each pullback bottoms higher than the last low. This is the signature of a healthy bull market.

Example — Titan Company

Titan makes a high at ₹3,200 → pulls back to ₹3,000 → rallies to ₹3,400 → pulls back to ₹3,150. Each high and low is higher. The trend is your friend — only look for BUY setups.

2

Lower Highs & Lower Lows = Downtrend

Each rally attempt falls short of the previous peak, and each decline goes lower than the last trough. Sellers are systematically in control.

Example — Paytm (2022)

After its IPO, Paytm made consistently lower highs and lower lows. Every rally was an opportunity to sell. A price action trader would refuse to buy here — always wait for the trend to change before fighting it.

3

Sideways / Consolidation = Range-Bound

Price oscillates between two roughly equal levels — no clear direction. In ranges, you buy at support and sell at resistance, but beware of false breakouts.

Example — Gold (₹57,000–₹60,000 range)

Gold spent weeks consolidating between ₹57,000 (support) and ₹60,000 (resistance). Savvy traders bought near ₹57,000 and sold near ₹60,000 repeatedly, accumulating small profits while waiting for the big breakout.

4

Trend Break = Change of Character (ChoCh)

The first sign of a trend reversal is when price breaks the last significant swing low (in uptrend) or last swing high (in downtrend). This is your earliest warning signal.

Advanced Concept

If an uptrend breaks its most recent higher low for the first time — that "change of character" is your cue to tighten stops, take profits, or prepare for a potential reversal trade. Many professionals use this as their primary exit signal.

Chapter 06

Entry, Stop-Loss & Targets

A pattern alone is not a trade. A complete trade requires three elements: where to enter, where you are wrong (stop-loss), and where to take profit (target).

Element What It Is Rule of Thumb Example (Stock Trade)
Entry Point Where you open the trade Enter on pattern confirmation (next candle close) Hammer forms at ₹1,600 support; buy at open of next candle ₹1,615
Stop-Loss Where you admit you're wrong Place just below the signal candle's low Hammer low was ₹1,572; stop at ₹1,568 (₹47 risk)
Take Profit Where you lock in gains Minimum 2:1 reward-to-risk ratio Risk ₹47 → target at least ₹94 profit → TP at ₹1,709
Risk:Reward Ratio of potential gain vs. loss Minimum 2:1, ideally 3:1 Even with 40% win rate, you profit with 2:1 RR
Position Size How many shares/contracts to buy Risk only 1–2% of account per trade ₹5L account × 1% = ₹5,000 max risk ÷ ₹47 SL = 106 shares
Chapter 07

Multi-Timeframe Analysis

Professional traders never look at just one timeframe. They "zoom out to zoom in" — using a higher timeframe to understand the big picture, then drilling down to find precise entries.

🌍
WEEKLY

Overall trend direction. Are we in a bull or bear market? This is the "country-level" view.

🏙️
DAILY

Key support/resistance zones, major patterns. The "city-level" trading map.

🔍
4H / 1H

Find the precise entry pattern. Buy/sell signals with tight stop-loss levels.

⚡
15M / 5M

Fine-tune entry for day traders. Only use after higher TF confirms direction.

🎯 The "Top-Down" Workflow

Step 1 (Weekly): Nifty 50 is in a clear uptrend — higher highs, higher lows. Bias = bullish only.
Step 2 (Daily): Nifty pulled back to a major support at 21,500 with a bullish engulfing candle.
Step 3 (1H): A hammer + inside bar forms right at that 21,500 level at 10 AM.
Action: All three timeframes align. Enter long at the inside bar breakout. This is called "confluence" — the more timeframes agree, the higher the probability.

Chapter 08

The Pre-Trade Checklist

Before entering ANY trade, run through this checklist. Professionals don't skip this step — ever.

  • 1
    Trend Confirmed? — On the higher timeframe (daily/weekly), have I identified the primary direction? Am I trading with the trend, not against it?
  • 2
    Key Level Present? — Is there a meaningful support (for buys) or resistance (for sells) level here? Random entries away from key levels are low-quality.
  • 3
    Signal Candle Clear? — Is there a clear price action signal (pin bar, engulfing, inside bar, doji) at this level? Has it closed, or am I anticipating?
  • 4
    Stop-Loss Placed? — Do I know exactly where I am wrong? Is the stop logical (below key level/wick), not just a fixed number?
  • 5
    Risk:Reward ≥ 2:1? — Is my potential profit at least 2× my risk? If the target is too close or the stop too wide, the setup is not worth taking.
  • 6
    Position Sized Correctly? — Am I risking no more than 1–2% of my total account on this single trade? No matter how confident I am, this rule never changes.
  • 7
    News Risk? — Are there major news events (RBI policy, earnings, Fed meeting) in the next few hours that could override all technical analysis? If yes, wait or reduce size.
Chapter 09

Common Beginner Mistakes

Learning what NOT to do is just as important as learning techniques. These are the mistakes that cost beginners the most money.

❌ Over-Trading

Feeling the need to always be in a trade. The market doesn't owe you action. Waiting for A+ setups and doing nothing otherwise is a strategy.

Fix

Set a rule: max 2–3 trades per day. If you don't see a clear setup by noon, close your charts and come back tomorrow.

❌ Moving Your Stop-Loss

When price approaches your stop, the emotional temptation is to move it further away to avoid the loss. This is how small losses become account-killing losses.

Fix

Treat your stop-loss as sacred. You placed it at a logical level when you were calm. Don't move it when you're emotional. Accept the loss — it's the cost of doing business.

❌ Chasing Price

Entering after a big move has already happened because you fear missing out. You buy at the top, the move reverses, and you're trapped in a losing position.

Fix

If you missed the move, let it go. Another setup will form. The market always gives you another opportunity. Patience is your edge.

❌ Ignoring the Trend

Looking for buy signals in a strong downtrend or sell signals in a strong uptrend. You're fighting the institutions with a retail account.

Fix

Always identify the trend first. In uptrends, only look for buy setups. In downtrends, only look for sell setups. With-trend trades have 60–70% higher probability.

Summary

Your Price Action Roadmap

You've now covered the full foundation of professional price action trading. Here's your learning path forward:

WEEK 1–2: FOUNDATIONS

Study candlestick anatomy. Identify support/resistance on 10 different charts. Do this every day without trading.

WEEK 3–4: PATTERNS

Learn to spot pin bars, engulfing candles, and inside bars at key levels. Mark them on charts retrospectively before trading them live.

MONTH 2: PAPER TRADE

Execute trades on a demo account using real-time charts. Journal every trade: setup, entry, stop, target, outcome, lesson.

MONTH 3+: LIVE (SMALL)

Start with minimal capital. Focus on executing the process, not on profits. Increase size only after 50+ trades of consistency.

Single Candlestick Patterns

🔨 Hammer

Bullish Reversal Beginner Friendly
Hammer at Support → Reversal
🧠 What's Happening (Psychology)

Price is falling. Sellers push it way down. But then — buyers flood in like a rescue team. They push price all the way back up, almost erasing the entire down-move. The long bottom wick is the "battle scar" of sellers trying and failing.

Bears push down → Bulls rescue → Reversal likely
🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Imagine a rubber ball. Someone throws it hard at the floor (sellers). But the floor bounces it back up strongly (buyers at support). The hammer's long wick IS that bounce.

🎯 How to Trade It
Wait for the hammer to fully close (don't jump early)
Enter when the NEXT candle closes above the hammer's high
Stop-loss: just below the hammer's lowest wick point
Target: next resistance level (2:1 or 3:1 ratio)
⚠️ Common Mistakes

A hammer in the middle of nowhere is unreliable. It only works powerfully when it appears at a key support level. Always check the context first — is there a meaningful floor below?

🌠 Shooting Star

Bearish Reversal Beginner Friendly
Shooting Star at Resistance → Reversal
🧠 Psychology

Price is rising. Bulls get excited and push price way up (the long upper wick). But bears were waiting at resistance. They dump hard, pushing price all the way back down near where it opened. Bulls tried to fly — bears shot them down.

Bulls surge up → Bears reject → Sell signal
🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Imagine throwing a ball straight up (buyers push price high). The ceiling stops it (resistance) and gravity pulls it back down (sellers). The shooting star's upper wick is that ceiling in action.

🎯 How to Trade It
Only trade at a confirmed resistance zone
Enter SHORT when next candle closes below the star's body low
Stop-loss: above the star's highest wick point
Target: next support level below
⚠️ Key Rule

The upper wick must be at least 2× the body size. A small upper wick is just a normal candle. The long rejection wick is what gives this pattern its power.

✝️ Doji

Indecision / Reversal Intermediate
4 Types of Doji
🧠 Psychology

A doji means buyers and sellers fought all session long — and neither won. Price opened and closed at nearly the same level. It's the market saying "I can't decide." This pause often comes right before a big move.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Think of a tug-of-war where both teams are equally strong. The rope (price) barely moves. Eventually one side gets tired — that's when the big move happens after the doji.

🎯 How to Trade It
Never trade a doji in isolation — wait for the candle AFTER it
Dragonfly Doji at support + next candle green = BUY signal
Gravestone Doji at resistance + next candle red = SELL signal
The "confirmation candle" is your actual entry trigger
Two-Candle Patterns

🟢 Bullish Engulfing

Strong Bullish Reversal Beginner Friendly
Bullish Engulfing at Support
🧠 Psychology

The first (red) candle continues the downtrend — bears still in control. Then suddenly, a massive green candle swallows the entire red candle completely. This means institutions stepped in with overwhelming force. The power shift is decisive and visible to everyone on the chart.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Day 1: Small gang (bears) wins the street fight. Day 2: An entire army (bulls) shows up and completely dominates. The bigger the green candle vs. the red one, the stronger the institutional buying.

🎯 How to Trade It
Enter BUY at the close of the large green candle (or open of next)
Stop-loss: below the lowest point of the entire two-candle pattern
Target: 2–3× the risk distance above your entry
Bonus: Even stronger if volume spikes on the green candle
⚠️ Rule to Remember

The green candle MUST open at or below the red candle's close AND close above the red candle's open. Partial engulfing doesn't count — it must be complete.

🔴 Bearish Engulfing

Strong Bearish Reversal Beginner Friendly
Bearish Engulfing at Resistance
🧠 Psychology

The mirror image of bullish engulfing. Bulls push price up (small green), then a massive red candle smothers everything bulls did, and more. Big sellers (institutions) are distributing — selling large positions at resistance while retail buyers are still optimistic.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Imagine you're pushing a heavy door open (bulls). Just as you make a small gap — someone on the other side pushes it completely shut and then some (bears). That's bearish engulfing.

🎯 How to Trade It
Enter SHORT at the close of the large red candle
Stop-loss: above the highest wick of the two-candle pattern
Target: next support level below for profit

📦 Inside Bar

Continuation / Breakout Intermediate
Inside Bar = Coiling Energy
🧠 Psychology

After a strong move (the mother bar), the market pauses and consolidates into a tiny range (inside bar). Both bulls and bears are quiet — it's the calm before the storm. When price finally breaks out of the mother bar's range, it usually moves strongly in that direction.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Think of a spring being coiled tight. The inside bar is the coiling phase. When the spring releases (breakout), the energy is explosive and fast. The tighter the inside bar, the more powerful the breakout.

🎯 How to Trade It
In uptrend: place a BUY STOP order just above the mother bar's HIGH
In downtrend: place a SELL STOP order just below the mother bar's LOW
Stop-loss: on the opposite side of the mother bar
This is great for trending markets — don't use in choppy sideways conditions
Chart & Structure Patterns

🏔️🏔️ Double Top

Major Bearish Reversal Intermediate
Double Top → Sell on Neckline Break
🧠 Psychology

Price hits a high, pulls back, tries again — and fails at the same level. The second failure is critical: it confirms that sellers are strongly defending this level. Big money sold at top 1 and again at top 2. When the "neckline" (middle low) breaks, it triggers a flood of sell orders.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

You try to jump over a wall (resistance). You fail. You try again from the same spot. You fail again. After two attempts, you give up and retreat — that's the price breaking down below the neckline.

🎯 How to Trade It
Enter SHORT when price closes BELOW the neckline level
Stop-loss: above the second top (above resistance)
Target: measure the height of the pattern from top to neckline, project downward
Works best after a long uptrend — the bigger the prior rally, the bigger the reversal

🏕️🏕️ Double Bottom

Major Bullish Reversal Intermediate
Double Bottom → Buy on Neckline Break
🧠 Psychology

Price falls to a low and bounces. Falls again to the same low and bounces again. Two attempts by bears to push lower — both failed. Buyers are very strong at this level. When price breaks the middle high (neckline) upward, it's a confirmed reversal to the upside.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Like a ball bouncing off the floor twice at the same height. The floor (support) is very strong. After the second bounce, the ball goes much higher. That's your buy signal.

🎯 How to Trade It
Enter LONG when price closes ABOVE the neckline
Stop-loss: just below the second bottom (below support)
Target: measure height from bottom to neckline, add to neckline breakout point
📈 Bullish Continuation Patterns

These patterns form during an uptrend and signal that the bullish move will continue higher after a brief pause or consolidation.

📐 Bullish Ascending Triangle

Bullish Continuation Intermediate
Ascending Triangle → Buy on Breakout
🧠 What's Happening (Psychology)

Buyers are getting increasingly aggressive — they keep making higher lows (rising support line). Each pullback is shallower than the last. Meanwhile, sellers are defending a flat resistance. But with every attempt, buyers push harder. Eventually, buyers overwhelm sellers and price breaks above the flat top with force.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Imagine a crowd trying to push through a locked door. Each attempt starts higher than the last (rising floor level). Eventually, they push so hard the door breaks open — that's your breakout signal!

🎯 How to Trade It
Buy when a candle closes clearly ABOVE the flat resistance line
Stop-loss: just below the last higher low (rising support line)
Target: height of the triangle added to the breakout point
Best used in strong uptrends — a "pause before continuation"
📌 Real Example

Reliance Industries (NSE): After a strong rally in 2023, Reliance formed an ascending triangle consolidation near ₹2,600 resistance for 6 weeks. When it broke above ₹2,600 on high volume, the stock rallied an additional ₹180+ in 10 days. Entry ₹2,610, SL ₹2,540, Target ₹2,790 — a clean 2.5:1 R:R setup.

🚩 Bullish Flag

Bullish Continuation Beginner Friendly
Bull Flag: Strong Pole + Tight Pullback
🧠 Psychology

Price surges strongly upward (the flagpole) — this is institutions buying aggressively. Then price drifts down in a calm, orderly channel (the flag). This pullback is NOT selling pressure — it's profit-taking by weak hands. The strong hands (institutions) hold their positions. When the flag breaks upward, the smart money adds more — and the move continues.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

A sprinter runs hard (flagpole), then jogs slowly to catch their breath (flag). When the whistle blows again (breakout), they sprint at full speed. The rest was just recovery — not defeat.

🎯 How to Trade It
Buy when price breaks ABOVE the upper boundary of the flag channel
Stop-loss: below the lower boundary of the flag (recent swing low)
Target: add the flagpole length to the breakout point
Volume should DRY UP during the flag and SPIKE on breakout — confirmation!
📌 Real Example

TCS (NSE), 2023: TCS gapped up ₹280 in one session after strong quarterly results (flagpole). For the next 2 weeks, it pulled back ₹90 in a tight declining channel (flag). On volume breakout above the channel, it ran ₹320 higher — almost equal to the original flagpole height. Entry ₹3,620, SL ₹3,530, Target ₹3,940.

📉 Bullish Falling Wedge (Continuation)

Bullish Continuation Intermediate
Falling Wedge in Uptrend = Bullish
🧠 Psychology

Price pulls back against the uptrend but in a narrowing, converging channel. Both the highs and lows are declining, but the lows are declining FASTER — meaning sellers are running out of steam. When price breaks above the upper declining trendline, bulls take full control again.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Like water going down a funnel — the pressure builds the narrower it gets. When it exits the funnel (breakout), it shoots out with force. The tighter the wedge, the more explosive the breakout.

🎯 How to Trade It
Buy on a strong candle close ABOVE the upper declining trendline
Stop-loss: below the last swing low inside the wedge
Target: top of the wedge (measured move) or the prior high
More reliable when the wedge forms on declining volume
📌 Real Example

Nifty 50 (Jan 2024): After a strong rally to 22,100, Nifty corrected in a falling wedge to 21,200 over 3 weeks. The breakout above the upper wedge line triggered a run to 22,500. Entry at 21,900, SL at 21,400, Target 22,500 = clean 2.4:1 R:R.

🔺 Bullish Symmetrical Triangle

Bullish Continuation Intermediate
Symmetrical Triangle → Bullish Breakout
🧠 Psychology

Neither bulls nor bears are winning — price makes lower highs AND higher lows, converging to a point (apex). This is a battle of indecision. In an uptrend, the prior momentum usually wins — price breaks upward. The longer the coiling, the bigger the breakout move.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Two armies pushing against each other with equal force — the standoff gets smaller and smaller (converging). Eventually, one side gains the upper hand and the other retreats completely. In an uptrend, bulls usually win this standoff.

🎯 How to Trade It
In uptrend: buy breakout above the upper declining trendline
Stop-loss: below the lower trendline of the triangle
Target: add the height of the triangle's base to the breakout point
⚠ Caution: breakout can go EITHER way — wait for confirmation before entering
📌 Real Example

Infosys (NSE): After a bullish trend, Infosys formed a symmetrical triangle over 5 weeks near ₹1,500. When it broke above the upper line on strong volume, it rallied ₹150 within 2 weeks, nearly matching the triangle's height. Classic continuation in a bull market.

🔄 Bullish Reversal Patterns

These patterns form at the end of a downtrend and signal a potential bullish reversal — a shift from bearish to bullish momentum.

🙃 Inverted Head & Shoulders

Major Bullish Reversal Advanced
Inv. H&S → Buy on Neckline Break
🧠 Psychology

Price makes three lows — two shallower "shoulders" with a deeper "head" in the middle. Each time price tries to make new lows, buyers absorb more and more selling. When price breaks above the neckline, it signals bears have exhausted themselves. This is one of the most powerful and reliable reversal patterns in trading.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

Think of sellers punching a wall three times. First punch makes a dent (left shoulder). Second punch makes a bigger dent (head). Third punch barely makes a mark (right shoulder). The wall (buyers) is winning — and when sellers give up, price shoots up.

🎯 How to Trade It
Enter LONG when price closes ABOVE the neckline with strength
Stop-loss: below the right shoulder low
Target: add the head's depth to the neckline breakout point
Bonus: wait for a retest of neckline as support for safer entry
📌 Real Example

Bank Nifty (2022): After a prolonged downtrend, Bank Nifty formed a perfect Inverted H&S with head at 33,800, shoulders at ~35,500. Neckline at 37,000. Breakout above 37,000 led to a 2,200 point rally within 3 weeks — textbook measured move matching the head-to-neckline distance.

🏕️🏕️🏕️ Triple Bottom

Strong Bullish Reversal Intermediate
Triple Bottom → 3 Tests of Support
🧠 Psychology

Price tests the same support level THREE times and bounces each time. This is extremely significant — it means buyers are incredibly strong at this level. Each failed attempt by bears strengthens the conviction of bulls. On the third bounce, professional traders recognize the pattern and institutional buying accelerates.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

A boxer trying to knock you down swings three times. You block all three. After the third miss, the boxer is tired — you go on offense. That's exactly what happens: bears swing three times, fail, and then bulls counter-attack powerfully.

🎯 How to Trade It
Enter on the third bounce from support — or on neckline breakout above
Stop-loss: just below the support zone (below all three bottoms)
Target: neckline breakout + pattern height (measured move)
Triple bottom is STRONGER than double bottom — three tests = very strong buyers
📌 Real Example

SBI (NSE, 2020 recovery): SBI bottomed around ₹149 three times between May-August 2020. Each bounce was stronger. When it broke above ₹195 neckline, the stock ran to ₹320+ over the next several months — a 60%+ move from the triple bottom breakout.

📉 Bearish Continuation Patterns

These patterns form during a downtrend and signal that the bearish move will continue lower after a brief pause or relief bounce.

🔻 Bearish Descending Triangle

Bearish Continuation Intermediate
Descending Triangle → Sell on Breakdown
🧠 Psychology

Sellers are getting more aggressive — price makes lower highs while buyers desperately defend a flat support. Each rally attempt is weaker than the last. Eventually, even the flat support fails, and there's nothing to stop the sell-off. Buyers run out of firepower.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

A dam holding back water with cracks getting bigger. Each wave of water hits the dam harder (sellers getting more aggressive). The dam (support) eventually breaks — and the flood (sell-off) is unstoppable.

🎯 How to Trade It
Sell SHORT when a candle closes BELOW the flat support level
Stop-loss: above the last lower high (upper declining line)
Target: height of triangle subtracted from the breakdown point
Works best in strong downtrends — a "pause before more selling"
📌 Real Example

Zomato (NSE, 2022): During its prolonged downtrend, Zomato formed a descending triangle near ₹60 support over 4 weeks. When ₹60 broke on heavy volume, the stock dropped to ₹44 within 2 weeks — a 27% move. Entry on breakdown: ₹59, SL ₹68, Target ₹44.

🚩 Bearish Flag

Bearish Continuation Beginner Friendly
Bear Flag: Sharp Drop + Weak Bounce
🧠 Psychology

Price crashes hard (flagpole) — institutional selling. Then a weak bounce/drift upward (flag) occurs as retail traders buy the dip, hoping for a recovery. But institutions are waiting — they use this bounce to add more short positions. When the flag breaks down, the next leg of selling is often just as violent as the flagpole drop.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

A stone is thrown off a cliff (sharp drop). It bounces once on a ledge (the flag — brief recovery). Then it continues falling to the bottom. Don't mistake the bounce as a reversal — it's just a pause on the way down.

🎯 How to Trade It
Sell SHORT on breakdown BELOW the lower boundary of the flag channel
Stop-loss: above the upper boundary of the flag
Target: add the flagpole length downward from the breakdown point
Volume dries up during flag, spikes on breakdown = high conviction signal
📌 Real Example

Paytm/One97 (NSE, 2022): After poor quarterly results, Paytm dropped ₹200 in 3 days (flagpole). A 2-week sideways-to-slightly-up drift followed (flag). When the flag broke down, another ₹180 drop followed — matching the original drop. Classic bear flag in a trending down stock.

🔻 Bearish Symmetrical Triangle

Bearish Continuation Intermediate
Symmetrical Triangle → Bearish Breakout
🧠 Psychology

Same as bullish symmetrical, but in a downtrend — prior momentum (bearish) usually wins. The indecision phase ends with bears overpowering bulls. In a downtrend, this pattern typically resolves downward. Context is everything — the same pattern in different trend directions signals different outcomes.

🎯 How to Trade It
In downtrend: sell SHORT on breakdown below the lower trendline
Stop-loss: above the upper declining trendline
Target: base height of triangle subtracted from breakdown point
⚠ ALWAYS wait for confirmed candle close outside the triangle — fake breakouts are common
📌 Real Example

Nifty IT Index (2022): During the tech selloff, Nifty IT formed a 6-week symmetrical triangle in a clear downtrend. The bearish breakdown led to a further 12% decline over the next month — a textbook bearish continuation setup.

📈 Bearish Rising Wedge (Continuation)

Bearish Continuation Intermediate
Rising Wedge in Downtrend = Bearish
🧠 Psychology

Price bounces in what appears to be a recovery — making higher highs AND higher lows. But the highs are rising more slowly than the lows (converging lines). This is a weak, exhausted rally with declining momentum. When it breaks down, bears who had been waiting pounce aggressively.

🎯 How to Trade It
Sell SHORT on breakdown BELOW the lower rising trendline
Stop-loss: above the last swing high inside the wedge
Target: bottom of the wedge or previous support levels below
RSI divergence (lower RSI despite higher price) confirms weakness
📌 Real Example

Adani Group stocks (Jan 2023): After the Hindenburg report selloff, several Adani stocks formed rising wedges during brief recovery attempts. These wedges all broke down, continuing the primary downtrend — confirming the rising wedge as a bearish continuation signal in a downtrend context.

🔄 Bearish Reversal Patterns

These patterns form at the end of an uptrend and signal a potential bearish reversal — a shift from bullish to bearish momentum.

🏔️ Head & Shoulders

Major Bearish Reversal Advanced
H&S → Sell on Neckline Break
🧠 Psychology

Bulls make one high (left shoulder), then an even higher high (head), then a LOWER high (right shoulder). Each rally attempt is weaker. Big money is distributing — selling to eager retail buyers at these highs. When the neckline breaks, the selling avalanche begins. This is the single most reliable major reversal pattern in all of trading.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

A marathon runner at the end of a race. First sprint (left shoulder) — strong. Final surge (head) — peak effort. Then one more push (right shoulder) — noticeably weaker. After the right shoulder, they collapse (neckline break). The exhaustion is visible in the price structure.

🎯 How to Trade It
Enter SHORT when price closes BELOW the neckline convincingly
Stop-loss: above the right shoulder high
Target: measure head-to-neckline distance, subtract from neckline breakout
Classic retest: price often bounces back to neckline as resistance — add there too
📌 Real Example

Nifty 50 (Oct 2021): Nifty formed a textbook Head & Shoulders near the 18,600 top. Left shoulder at 17,800, Head at 18,604, Right shoulder at 18,200, Neckline at 17,500. The neckline break triggered a 1,500+ point correction to 16,000. Measured move: 18,604 − 17,500 = 1,104 subtracted from 17,500 = target ~16,396. Remarkably accurate.

📈 Rising Wedge (Reversal)

Bearish Reversal Intermediate
Rising Wedge at Top → Bearish Reversal
🧠 Psychology

Price is rising, but the gains are getting smaller with each move — momentum is dying. Both the highs and lows are rising, but they're converging. Buyers are losing steam. Sellers see this weakness and begin pressing harder. The final break below the lower rising trendline signals that buyers have completely given up.

🎯 How to Trade It
Sell SHORT when price breaks BELOW the lower rising trendline
Stop-loss: above the last high inside the wedge (near the apex)
Target: beginning of the wedge (where it started) as first target
Even stronger when it forms after a long uptrend and at a major resistance level
📌 Real Example

Gold (MCX, mid-2023): Gold formed a rising wedge on the daily chart after a rally from ₹54,000 to ₹60,000. The wedge breakdown signaled the reversal, and gold retraced back toward ₹56,500 over the following weeks — nearly retracing the entire wedge's rise. Classic reversal signal at a major high.

🏔️🏔️🏔️ Triple Top

Strong Bearish Reversal Intermediate
Triple Top → 3 Failures at Resistance
🧠 Psychology

Price hits the SAME resistance level three times and gets rejected every single time. Sellers are incredibly powerful at this level — institutions are distributing (selling their large holdings) to retail buyers at the top. After three failures, even the most optimistic retail buyers give up, and the neckline collapses.

🙋 Analogy for Freshers

You apply for the same job three times and get rejected each time. After the third rejection, you stop trying. The company (resistance) clearly doesn't want you. Price behaves the same — after three rejections, it gives up trying to go higher and falls.

🎯 How to Trade It
Short on the third rejection from resistance — or on neckline breakdown
Stop-loss: above the resistance zone (above all three tops)
Target: neckline breakdown minus pattern height (measured move)
Triple top = STRONGER than double top — three failures = very powerful sellers
📌 Real Example

Nifty 50 (2023 high): Nifty tested the 19,800–19,900 zone three times over 6 weeks. Each attempt was rejected sharply. When the neckline at 19,200 broke, Nifty fell to 18,800 rapidly — an 1,100 point move nearly matching the height of the triple top pattern. A powerful macro-level pattern on the index.

Setups & Strategies

Professional Trade Setups

These are the exact setups professional traders look for every day. Each setup has a clear entry, stop-loss, and target. Study them until you can spot them instantly on any chart.

Bullish A+ Setup

Setup #1 — Pin Bar Bounce at Support

★★★★★
WIN RATE ~65%
Pin Bar Bounce → Buy Entry
📍 Entry Rules

Wait for the pin bar to fully close. Enter BUY at the open of the next candle. Aggressive traders enter at the close of the pin bar itself. The pin bar must have a lower wick at least 2× its body.

🛑 Stop-Loss Placement

Place stop-loss 5–10 pips/points below the pin bar's lowest wick. This is your invalidation point — if price goes there, your setup is wrong and you exit with a small loss.

🎯 Profit Target

Target the next resistance level above. Minimum 2:1 risk-reward. If support is at ₹1,600 and stop is ₹1,570 (₹30 risk), target must be at least ₹1,660 (₹60 profit).

✅ Confluence Checklist
1
Higher timeframe trend is UP (daily/weekly bullish)
2
Pin bar is at a known, horizontal support level
3
Wick is long (2×+ body), body closes near the top
4
No major news event in the next few hours
Bearish A+ Setup

Setup #2 — Bearish Engulfing at Weekly Resistance

★★★★★
WIN RATE ~68%
Bearish Engulf at Weekly Level
📍 Entry Rules

Enter SHORT at the close of the large red candle, or on the open of the next candle. The red candle must completely engulf the prior green candle's body — partial engulf doesn't qualify.

🛑 Stop-Loss Placement

Stop goes above the highest wick of the two-candle engulfing pattern. The weekly resistance should hold — if price closes above it, you're wrong.

🎯 Profit Target

First target: the most recent swing low (prior pullback level). Second target: the next major support zone below. Scale out — take 50% at T1, let the rest run to T2.

💡 Why Weekly Levels Work Best

Weekly levels are watched by institutions worldwide. When big money sells at a weekly resistance, the move is usually sharp and sustained — giving you excellent risk:reward with a tight stop.

Continuation Intermediate

Setup #3 — Inside Bar Breakout (Trend Continuation)

★★★★★
WIN RATE ~58%
Inside Bar → Breakout Long Entry
📍 Entry Rules

Place a buy stop order 1–2 points above the mother bar's HIGH before the market opens. When price breaks above it automatically, you're in — no need to watch the screen.

🛑 Stop-Loss Placement

Stop goes below the mother bar's LOW. For a tighter stop, place it below the inside bar's low (but this is riskier — can get stopped out by noise).

🎯 Profit Target

In a strong trend, the next swing high or round number resistance. Trail your stop using the most recent higher low as the trend continues — let winners run.

🧠 Pro Insight

The best inside bars form after a very large move (big mother bar). The bigger the mother bar, the more energy is coiled in the inside bar, and the more explosive the breakout tends to be.

Major Reversal Intermediate

Setup #4 — Double Bottom with Neckline Breakout

★★★★★
WIN RATE ~62%
Double Bottom → Buy Neckline Break
📍 Entry Rules

Do NOT enter at the second bottom — wait for confirmation. Enter BUY only when price closes above the neckline (the middle high between the two bottoms). This avoids false signals.

🛑 Stop-Loss Placement

Stop goes below the second bottom's low. If price falls back through support after the neckline break, the pattern has failed.

🎯 Measured Move Target

Classic technique: measure the distance from the bottoms to the neckline. Project that same distance upward from the neckline breakout point. This gives your minimum price target.

⏱ Patience Required

The two bottoms should form over several days or weeks on a daily chart. A pattern that forms in minutes on a 1-min chart is far less reliable. Higher timeframe = higher quality.

Mental Game

Trading Psychology

90% of traders lose money. Most have the right strategy — they fail on execution. Trading psychology is the final, hardest frontier that separates consistent winners from everyone else.

🧠

The Hard Truth About Trading

Your brain was NOT designed for trading. Every instinct you have — fear, greed, hope — works against you in the markets. Trading is about systematically overriding your primal brain with disciplined logic.

The 6 Emotional Enemies

What destroys traders — and how to fight back

😨

Fear of Loss

Causes you to exit winning trades too early ("lock in profits!") and hold losing trades too long ("it'll come back…").

Fix: Pre-set your stop and target before entering. Commit to not moving them. Let the market decide.
🤑

Greed

Moving your profit target further away as price approaches it. Turning a good trade into a loss because you wanted more.

Fix: Set your target at a logical level (resistance/measured move) and honour it. Take profit at your plan.
🏃

FOMO

Fear Of Missing Out. Chasing a trade that has already moved significantly — entering at the worst possible price.

Fix: Remind yourself: "There will always be another setup." Missed trades are invisible losses. Chased trades are real losses.
🎲

Revenge Trading

After a loss, immediately taking another trade to "win it back." Emotional, impulsive, undisciplined — and almost always results in a bigger loss.

Fix: After any loss, close your trading platform for at least 30 minutes. Walk away. Return only when calm.
🤯

Overconfidence

After a winning streak, increasing position size dramatically or ignoring risk rules. "I can't lose!" — right before a huge loss wipes out all gains.

Fix: Keep position size fixed as a % of account. Never change it because of emotions. Wins or losses — same size.
🙈

Hope & Denial

Holding a losing trade past your stop because you "hope" it will turn around. Hope is not a strategy. The market doesn't care about your feelings.

Fix: When price hits your stop — exit. Always. No exceptions. A small loss is a tuition fee. Ignoring your stop is gambling.

The Daily Routine

Build a Trading Routine

Professionals don't react to the market — they have a structured routine that removes emotion from the equation.

Pre-Market (30 min before open)

Mark key support/resistance levels on daily chart. Note any major news events. Write down your bias (bullish/bearish/neutral) for the day. Set price alerts.

Market Hours — Wait for Your Setup

Don't stare at the screen. Check every 15–30 minutes. Only act when a setup matches your pre-market plan exactly. If no setup — do nothing.

Trade Execution — Follow the Plan

Enter → set stop-loss → set target → walk away. Your job is done. Do NOT watch price tick by tick. Checking P&L every minute increases emotional decisions.

Post-Market Review (15 min)

Log every trade in your journal. Screenshot the chart. Note: what went right, what went wrong, did you follow your rules? This is where real improvement happens.

Weekly Review (30 min every weekend)

Review all trades of the week. Calculate win rate, average R:R, total P&L. Identify your best setups and worst mistakes. Adjust — never stop improving.

📏 The Golden Rules of Risk Management

  • ①Never risk more than 1-2% per trade. With a ₹5L account, that's ₹5,000–₹10,000 max risk. Even 10 consecutive losses won't blow your account.
  • ②Always target minimum 2:1 R:R. Even at a 40% win rate with 2:1 R:R, you're profitable over time. The math works in your favour.
  • ③Calculate position size before every trade. Never guess. Always know exactly how many shares/contracts you can buy given your risk parameters.
  • ④Adjust size for volatility. A stock moving 5% daily needs a wider stop — reduce position size accordingly so your rupee risk stays constant.
  • ⑤Daily loss limit: 3% of account max. If you hit 3% daily drawdown, stop trading for the day. Come back tomorrow with a fresh mind.
