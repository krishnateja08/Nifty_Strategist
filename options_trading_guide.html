01 · Foundation

What Are Options?

An option is a financial contract that gives the buyer the right — but NOT the obligation — to buy or sell an asset (like a stock) at a specific price, on or before a specific date.

Imagine you're interested in buying a house worth ₹50 Lakhs. You tell the seller: "I'll pay you ₹1 Lakh today for the right to buy this house at ₹50 Lakhs anytime in the next 3 months."

If the house price rises to ₹70 Lakhs in 2 months, you can still buy it at ₹50 Lakhs — making ₹20 Lakhs profit!
If the price drops, you simply walk away. You only lose the ₹1 Lakh you paid. That ₹1 Lakh = the Option Premium.

📜
CONTRACT

It's a Contract

An options contract is between a buyer and a seller. The buyer pays a premium to get the right. The seller receives the premium and takes on the obligation.

🎯
LEVERAGE

It's Leveraged

With a small amount of money (premium), you can control a much larger position in the underlying asset. This means massive potential gains — but also complete loss of your premium.

⏰
TIME

It Expires

Options are not forever. Every option has an expiry date. After that date, the contract is worthless if not exercised. Time works against buyers and for sellers.

🏦
ASSET

Underlying Asset

Options can be on stocks (like Reliance, Infosys), indexes (Nifty 50, Bank Nifty), commodities, or currencies. The asset your option is based on is called the underlying.

ℹ️
Key Insight: Right vs. Obligation The buyer has the RIGHT but not the obligation. The seller has the OBLIGATION to fulfil if the buyer exercises. This asymmetry is the core of options trading.

02 · Foundation

Call Options vs Put Options

There are only TWO types of options. Once you understand these, you understand 50% of options trading.

📞
CALL

Call Option

A CALL gives the buyer the right to BUY the underlying asset at the strike price. You buy a CALL when you believe the price will GO UP.

📈 Profit when price rises
📉 Loss limited to premium
🎯 Buyer is bullish
🤿
PUT

Put Option

A PUT gives the buyer the right to SELL the underlying asset at the strike price. You buy a PUT when you believe the price will GO DOWN.

📉 Profit when price falls
📈 Loss limited to premium
🎯 Buyer is bearish

CALL Analogy: You book a flight ticket from Delhi to Mumbai for ₹5,000 today for travel 2 months later. If ticket prices rise to ₹10,000, you still fly at ₹5,000. You called the price.

PUT Analogy: You have 100 kg of wheat. You're worried prices may fall. You pay a farmer ₹500 for the right to SELL your wheat at today's price of ₹2,000/kg for 3 months. You put the price floor in place.

03 · Key Terms

Essential Options Vocabulary

Before you trade a single option, you must know these terms cold. No shortcuts here.

🎯

Strike Price

The predetermined price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying asset. Example: Nifty 22,000 CE — strike is 22,000.

💵

Premium

The price you pay to buy an option. It's the cost of the contract. This is your maximum loss as a buyer. Example: Nifty 22,000 CE premium = ₹150 per lot.

📅

Expiry Date

The last date on which the option can be exercised. In India, weekly options expire every Thursday; monthly expire last Thursday. After expiry, options become worthless if out-of-the-money.

📦

Lot Size

Options are traded in lots, not individual units. Example: Nifty 1 lot = 50 units. So if premium is ₹150, you need ₹150 × 50 = ₹7,500 per lot.

📍

Spot Price

The current market price of the underlying asset right now. Example: Nifty is currently trading at 22,300 — that's the spot price.

🔄

Open Interest (OI)

Total number of outstanding option contracts that have NOT been settled or closed. High OI means high liquidity and strong interest at that strike. Very important for reading market sentiment.

📊

Volume

Number of contracts traded today. High volume = active market = easier to enter/exit. Always trade where there is good volume.

⚖️

Intrinsic Value vs Time Value

Intrinsic Value = Real value if exercised now. For a call: Spot − Strike (if positive).
Time Value = Premium − Intrinsic Value. It decays as expiry approaches (called Theta decay).

Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value. A far OTM option has ZERO intrinsic value — you're only paying for time and hope!

04 · Foundation

How Options Actually Work

Let's walk through a complete options trade from buying to profit booking with a real example.

1

You Analyse the Market

Nifty is at 22,000. You study charts, news, and believe Nifty will rise to 22,500 in the next 2 weeks (you are bullish).

2

You Select an Option

You decide to buy a Nifty 22,200 Call Option expiring next Thursday. The premium is ₹120 per unit. Lot size = 50 units. Total cost = ₹120 × 50 = ₹6,000.

3

You Place the Buy Order

You buy 1 lot of Nifty 22,200 CE at ₹120. The money (₹6,000) leaves your account as premium. This is the maximum you can lose.

4

Market Moves in Your Favour

2 days later, Nifty rises to 22,500. Your 22,200 CE premium has now risen to ₹320 because it's now deep in-the-money.

5

You Sell / Square Off

You sell the option at ₹320. Your profit = (₹320 − ₹120) × 50 = ₹200 × 50 = ₹10,000 profit on a ₹6,000 investment. That's +167% return!

05 · Options Chain

What is an Options Chain?

An options chain (also called option matrix) is a table that lists ALL available options for a particular stock or index — every strike price, every expiry, calls AND puts — all in one place.

Think of an options chain like a restaurant menu. The menu shows you all available dishes (strike prices), their prices (premium), how popular they are (volume/OI), and what category they fall in (call/put). You pick what suits your appetite (trade plan) and budget (capital).

📊

All Strikes

Shows every available strike price from deep ITM to deep OTM in one view.

🔄

Live Data

Updates in real time as the market moves. Premiums, OI, and volume change by the second.

🧭

Market Sentiment

High OI at certain strikes reveals where big players (institutions) have placed bets — crucial intelligence.

07 · Options Chain

In-The-Money, At-The-Money, Out-of-The-Money

These three terms describe the relationship between the current stock price and the strike price. They're absolutely crucial for every options trader.

Assume Nifty Spot Price = 22,100 for all examples below.
✅
ITM

In-The-Money

The option has real intrinsic value right now.

Call ITM: Strike < Spot Price
22,000 CE when Nifty=22,100 → ITM ✅

Put ITM: Strike > Spot Price
22,200 PE when Nifty=22,100 → ITM ✅

Higher premium. More expensive but safer bet.

🎯
ATM

At-The-Money

Strike price is equal or very close to the current spot price.

Call ATM: Strike ≈ Spot Price
22,100 CE when Nifty=22,100 → ATM 🎯

Put ATM: Strike ≈ Spot Price
22,100 PE when Nifty=22,100 → ATM 🎯

Most liquidity, most sensitive to price moves. Traders' favourite.

❌
OTM

Out-of-The-Money

The option has NO intrinsic value. Only time value.

Call OTM: Strike > Spot Price
22,400 CE when Nifty=22,100 → OTM ❌

Put OTM: Strike < Spot Price
21,800 PE when Nifty=22,100 → OTM ❌

Cheap premium. High risk. Like a lottery ticket.

08 · Pricing

How Are Options Priced?

Option premiums aren't random. They are determined by 6 key factors. Understanding these factors means you can predict how premiums will move.

FACTOR 1
📍

Spot Price vs Strike

The farther the spot is from the strike (in your favour), the higher the premium. An ITM option costs more than an OTM option.

FACTOR 2
⏳

Time to Expiry

More time = more premium. A 30-day option is more expensive than a 7-day option. As days pass, time value decays (Theta decay). This hurts buyers, helps sellers.

FACTOR 3
🌪️

Implied Volatility (IV)

Higher expected price swings = higher premium. Before earnings, events, or Budget, IV spikes and premiums become expensive. After the event, IV crashes (IV crush).

FACTOR 4
📈

Direction of Price Move

Call premiums rise when price goes up. Put premiums rise when price falls. This is the most obvious factor.

FACTOR 5
🏦

Risk-Free Interest Rate

Higher interest rates slightly increase call premiums and decrease put premiums. Less impactful for retail traders in short-term options.

FACTOR 6
💰

Dividends

For stock options, expected dividends reduce call premiums and increase put premiums slightly.

🔑
The Black-Scholes Model The most famous option pricing formula. It uses: Spot price, Strike price, Time to expiry, Implied Volatility, and Risk-free rate to calculate fair value. Most trading platforms compute this for you automatically.

09 · Greeks

The Option Greeks

Greeks measure how sensitive an option's price is to different factors. Master these and you become a true options professional. Each Greek is named after a Greek letter.

Δ
Delta
How much the option premium changes when the stock price moves by ₹1 (or 1 point).
Range: 0 to 1 (calls) / −1 to 0 (puts)

ATM option Delta ≈ 0.5 means if Nifty moves 100 pts, premium moves ~50 pts

Γ
Gamma
Rate of change of Delta. It tells you how fast Delta itself is changing as the stock price moves.
Highest near ATM, near expiry

High Gamma = option becomes very sensitive. A double-edged sword near expiry.

Θ
Theta
Time decay. How much the option's value decreases each passing day, all else equal.
Always negative for buyers!

If Theta = −5, your option loses ₹5 per day just from time passing. Enemy of buyers, friend of sellers.

Ν
Vega
Sensitivity to changes in Implied Volatility. A 1% increase in IV changes the option by Vega amount.
Positive for buyers

Buy options when IV is LOW. Sell options when IV is HIGH. Vega helps you exploit IV changes.

ρ
Rho
Sensitivity to changes in interest rates. Usually the least impactful Greek for short-term traders.
Least important for retail

More relevant for long-dated options (LEAPS). Can be safely ignored by most short-term option buyers.

📋
Summary Guide
📈 Price up → Delta profits
📅 Time passes → Theta hurts
🌪️ IV rises → Vega profits
💥 Big moves → Gamma gains

10 · Implied Volatility

Implied Volatility (IV) Deep Dive

IV is the single most important concept for options traders beyond price direction. It tells you whether options are cheap or expensive right now.

Think of IV like the price of umbrella insurance. During a sunshine week, umbrella insurance is cheap (low IV). The day before a predicted storm, it becomes very expensive (high IV). Smart traders buy before the storm is expected and sell just before it hits — not after.

🔥

High IV Environment

  • Premiums are expensive
  • Don't buy options — you overpay
  • Better to SELL options (collect fat premium)
  • Happens before events: Budget, RBI policy, earnings
  • After event, IV collapses (IV Crush)
🌊

Low IV Environment

  • Premiums are cheap
  • Good time to BUY options
  • Avoid selling — collect thin premium, high risk
  • Happens in quiet, sideways markets
  • Buy calls/puts and wait for volatility to spike
💣
IV Crush — The Beginner Trap! You buy a call before earnings. The company reports great results. The stock goes UP. But your option LOSES value. Why? Because IV was 80% before earnings and crashed to 30% after. The Vega loss exceeded the Delta gain. This is IV crush — one of the most expensive lessons new traders learn.
India VIX is India's "fear index." VIX above 20 = High IV / expensive options. VIX below 12 = Low IV / cheap options. Always check VIX before trading!

14 · Execution

How to Actually Trade Options

Here's your step-by-step checklist for placing your first (or next) options trade professionally.

1

Open a Demat + Trading Account

Use a broker that offers F&O trading: Zerodha (Kite), Upstox, Angel One, or similar. Get F&O segment activated. This requires signing additional forms and minimum capital requirements.

2

Form Your Market View

Never trade without a view. Ask: Is the market bullish, bearish, or neutral? What is the expected range? Where is support and resistance? What is the timeframe?

3

Check the Options Chain

Open the options chain. Check IV (is it high or low?). Find the ATM strike. Look at OI concentration. Choose your strike price based on your view and budget.

4

Select Expiry Date

Weekly (Thu) for short-term trades. Monthly for medium-term. Never hold short-dated OTM options too long — theta eats them alive in the last 2 days.

5

Calculate Position Size & Risk

Never risk more than 2-3% of your capital per trade. Calculate: if this option goes to zero, how much do I lose? That's your max risk per trade.

6

Place the Order

Use LIMIT orders (not market orders) to get better fills. Set the price at or slightly above the Ask for buying, slightly below Bid for selling.

7

Manage the Trade

Set a stop-loss target (e.g., 50% of premium paid). Set a profit target (e.g., 50-100% gain). Monitor greeks. Be ready to exit early if your view changes.

Trade Execution → Order Types
15 · Order Types

Options Order Types

Placing the wrong order type can cost you money. Know these cold before you trade.

🎯
BEST PRACTICE

Limit Order

You specify the exact price you want. Order fills only if market reaches your price. Always use limit orders in options — the bid-ask spread can be wide and market orders can get you terrible fills.

⚡
AVOID

Market Order

Fills immediately at whatever price is available. In options, this can result in overpaying significantly due to wide bid-ask spreads. Avoid except in extreme emergency exits.

🛑
PROTECTION

Stop-Loss Order (SL)

Automatically exits your position if the price reaches a loss threshold. Always set SL. Without it, you have no protection against catastrophic loss.

📋
ADVANCED

GTT (Good Till Triggered)

Set your profit target AND stop-loss in advance. The order stays active until triggered. Lets you trade without watching the screen all day.

💡
Bid-Ask Spread Explained In options, there's always a Bid price (what buyers offer) and Ask price (what sellers want). The gap between them is the spread. For example: Bid ₹118, Ask ₹122 = ₹4 spread. Always buy near Ask and sell near Bid to get fair fills.
Trade Execution → Risk Management
16 · Risk Management

Risk Management in Options

Risk management is what separates professional traders from gamblers. No matter how smart your analysis is, without risk management you will eventually blow up.

🎯

2% Rule

Never risk more than 2% of your total capital on any single trade. On ₹1 Lakh capital, max risk per trade = ₹2,000.

✂️

Stop-Loss Always

Set a stop-loss of 50% of premium paid as a starting rule. If you paid ₹120, exit if premium hits ₹60. No exceptions.

📊

Never Over-Leverage

Don't trade too many lots for your capital. A single lot of Nifty options can move ₹5,000 in minutes. Size appropriately.

📅

Avoid Last-Day OTM

Never hold OTM options on expiry day unless you have a very strong reason. Theta destroys them completely. Exit before expiry Thursday.

📰

Respect Events

Exit positions before major events (RBI, Fed, Budget, earnings) unless you specifically trade events. Surprises can wipe out months of profit.

🧮

Track Your Trades

Maintain a trade journal. Record entry, exit, reasoning, emotion, and outcome. This is how you find your edge over time.

🚨
The Option Seller's Risk When you sell options (especially naked/uncovered), your max profit is the premium received. But your max LOSS is theoretically unlimited (for calls) or very large (for puts). Always hedge short positions. Never sell options naked without experience and significant capital buffer.

10 Common Mistakes to Avoid

Learn from others' expensive mistakes. Every single one of these has wiped out beginner accounts.

❌
1. Buying cheap OTM options — They expire worthless 90% of the time. Just because they're cheap doesn't make them good.
❌
2. No stop-loss — "It'll come back." It usually doesn't. Always set a stop-loss before you enter.
❌
3. Holding till expiry — OTM options held till expiry almost always go to zero. Exit early and save capital.
❌
4. Trading around events without understanding IV crush — The IV trap is real. Many beginners lose money even being right on direction.
⚠️
5. Over-trading — Taking too many trades daily leads to emotional decisions and excessive brokerage. Quality over quantity.
⚠️
6. Revenge trading — After a loss, immediately trading to "recover" almost always compounds the loss. Take a break.
⚠️
7. Ignoring liquidity — Trading illiquid options with no volume means terrible fills and unable to exit when needed.
⚠️
8. Risking money you can't afford to lose — Options are high risk. Only trade with money that, if lost completely, won't affect your life.
💡
9. Not having a trade plan — Entry, target, stop-loss, reason — all defined BEFORE you enter. Not after.
💡
10. Skipping paper trading — Don't rush to real money. 3 months of paper trading will save you years of painful losses.

Complete Options Glossary

A reference guide to every important term in options trading. Bookmark this section.

ATM (At The Money)
Strike price equals or is very close to the current spot price.
ITM (In The Money)
Option with intrinsic value. Call: strike < spot. Put: strike > spot.
OTM (Out of The Money)
Option with no intrinsic value. Only time value remains.
CE (Call European)
Indian notation for Call Option. Can only be exercised at expiry.
PE (Put European)
Indian notation for Put Option. Can only be exercised at expiry.
Premium
Price paid to buy an option contract. Buyer's maximum loss.
Strike Price
Pre-agreed price at which option can be exercised.
Expiry Date
Last day option can be exercised. Weekly = Thursday in India.
Lot Size
Minimum number of units in 1 options contract. Nifty = 50 units.
Open Interest (OI)
Total unsettled contracts. Indicates market interest and liquidity.
Delta
Change in option price per 1-point move in underlying.
Theta
Daily time decay. How much premium is lost per day.
Vega
Sensitivity to implied volatility changes.
Gamma
Rate of change of Delta. Acceleration of price sensitivity.
Implied Volatility (IV)
Market's expectation of future price movement. Affects premiums.
India VIX
India's fear index. Measures expected volatility. High VIX = fear.
IV Crush
Sudden drop in IV after a major event, reducing option premiums.
PCR (Put-Call Ratio)
OI of puts / OI of calls. Above 1.2 = bearish sentiment.
Max Pain
Strike at which maximum number of options expire worthless.
Square Off
Closing your position by doing the opposite trade before expiry.
Assignment
When an option seller is obligated to buy/sell the underlying.
Exercise
Buyer using their right to buy/sell at strike price.
Naked Option
Selling options without a hedge. Unlimited risk. Dangerous.
Hedge
An offsetting position that reduces risk of another position.
Intrinsic Value
Real immediate value of an option if exercised right now.
Time Value (Extrinsic)
Premium beyond intrinsic value. Decays to zero at expiry.
F&O Margin
Capital required to sell options. Buying only needs premium amount.
SPAN Margin
Minimum margin required by exchange for option selling positions.
